INRODUCTION

Lithium hydride is a material which can be used for hydrogen storage and as a Li+ ion conductor. Mostly for application purpose, LiH is used as a precursor to make complex hydrides. This section focuses on the solid-state Li NMR studies on Li+ dynamics in such Li-M-hydrides, where M is usually Mg, B etc. For LiH doped with MgH2, it was found that above 573 K, the SLR rate is exclusively determined by the dipole–dipole interactions, indicating the motional behavior. The jump rate of H+ exceeded that of Li+ by far in the whole temperature range. The activation energy for Li+ diffusion was estimated as 0.55 eV from SLR NMR methods [56]. In LiBH4 a rapid reorientation of the BH 4 − unit in the LT phase (orthorhombic) was studied by solid-state NMR relaxometry [57]. Matsuo et al. studied the HT phase (hexagonal) of LiBH4 with NMR methods and observed very fast Li+ ionic diffusion [58]. The structural transition from LT to HT phase happens at 390 K. Slightly above 390 K, in the HT phase, a significant line narrowing occurs, showing the central and satellite transitions of the 7Li signal clearly. The 7Li T1 times of the HT phase show a diffusion-induced minimum at 493 K, and it was fitted using the BPP formula. The activation energy for Li+ diffusion was estimated to be 0.56 eV. It was concluded that the high electrical conductivity (10−3 S cm−1) of the HT phase is a result of the Li+ superionic conduction [58]. Immediately after reaching the HT phase the 1H NMR shows only small line narrowing due to Li ion motion. But no prominent hydrogen diffusion occurs until 443 K. Therefore the Li motion is much faster than the hydrogen diffusion in the HT phase [59, 60]. Later it was proposed that the reason for fast Li diffusion in the HT phase is due to the dimensionality (lower than 3D) of the diffusion pathway [61]. With the help of frequency-dependent 6/7Li SLR measurements, it was shown that the HT phase diffusion between the BH4 layers is 2D and that in the LT phase is 3D. The activation energy calculated for Li diffusion from the variable-temperature SLR rates was 0.22 eV [61]. Mixtures of LiBH4 and lithium halides helped to reduce the transition temperature and to increase the Li+ conductivity. This was first shown in a solid solution of LiBH4 and LiCl using NMR motional narrowing [62]. In another study, a considerable 7Li line narrowing and lowering of the transition temperature were observed for a solid solution of LiBH4 and LiI [63]. When confined in porous carbon and silicon, LiBH4 showed motional narrowing even at room temperature. The nanoconfinement enhanced the mobility of both Li+ and B H 4 − ions by stabilizing the HT phase in nanopores already at low temperatures [64, 65]. While bulk LiBH4 showed a Li+ jump rate of 109 s−1 at 453 K, the compound confined in the pores of carbon aerogel nanoscaffolds showed the same jump rate at 448 K, as estimated from the SLR rate peak [64]. The stabilization of the HT phase at lower temperatures was also achieved by nanostructuring of LiBH4 itself as well as in a composite with alumina. This was observed from the 6/7Li line narrowing at low temperatures. While the activation energy from the HT flank of the SLR peak of bulk LiBH4 was estimated to be approximately 0.5 eV, that for micro- and nano-LiBH4 was 0.40 eV, and that for micro- and nano-LiBH4–Al2O3 composite was 0.35 eV [66]. Two Li diffusion processes were observed in LiZn2(BH4)5 with activation energies 0.14 and 0.33 eV deduced from the SLR peaks. The temperature-dependent change of the 7Li NMR line shape is shown in Fig 2. The anisotropic BH4 reorientations were not strong enough to average the 7Li quadrupole interaction at 380–390 K [67]. In LiLa(BH4)3Cl, the Li+ ion jumps and the slow reorientations of B H 4 − groups were found to be correlated. An activation energy of 0.30 eV and a diffusion coefficient of 5.2 × 10−8 cm2 s−1 (at 297 K) were calculated from the 7Li SLR data [68].
